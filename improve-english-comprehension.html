Paper 2 · Comprehension · 20 Marks

How to Improve English Comprehension for PSLE

Comprehension is the highest-scoring single component in Paper 2 at 20 marks. This guide explains the three levels of questions and the strategies that consistently produce better answers.

📋 2025 SEAB syllabus aligned 🎯 10 questions · 20 marks

Why Comprehension Matters Most in Paper 2

The Comprehension section carries 20 marks — the highest of any single open-ended component in Paper 2. With 10 questions and marks ranging from 1 to 3 per question, it rewards students who can read carefully, think beyond the surface, and express their understanding in clear, complete sentences.

According to the 2025 SEAB syllabus, comprehension questions test three levels of understanding:

🔍
Literal
The answer is directly stated in the passage. Find and lift it accurately.
"According to the passage, what did the boy do when he heard the news?"
💡
Inferential
The answer is implied but not stated. You must read between the lines.
"What does this suggest about how the character was feeling?"
🧠
Evaluative
You must judge, assess, or give a personal response supported by evidence.
"Do you think the character made the right decision? Explain with reference to the passage."

Strategies for Each Question Type

Literal Fact-based questions 1–2 marks

The answer is in the passage. Your job is to find the exact section and lift it accurately — but do not copy an entire paragraph. Identify the specific detail the question asks for.

Strategy: Underline the key words in the question → locate the relevant paragraph → find the sentence with the answer → paraphrase slightly if needed (avoid copying word-for-word unless quoting for a vocabulary question). Answer in a complete sentence.
Inferential Reading between the lines 2–3 marks

The answer is not directly written. You need to use clues in the text — word choices, actions, descriptions — to draw a logical conclusion. These questions often ask about a character's feelings, motives, or the meaning of a phrase.

Strategy: Re-read the relevant paragraph slowly. Ask: What does this detail imply? What can I conclude from these actions or words? Use evidence from the text to support your inference. Do not bring in outside knowledge — all evidence must come from the passage.
Vocabulary-in-context Meaning of words and phrases 1–2 marks

These questions ask what a word or phrase means as used in the passage. The general dictionary definition may not be the intended meaning here.

Strategy: Re-read the sentence and the ones around it. Use context clues — what situation is being described? Try substituting your answer back into the sentence to check it makes sense. Avoid synonyms that change the tone or register (e.g., replacing "devastated" with "sad" loses the intensity).
Evaluative Personal response with text evidence 2–3 marks

These are the most challenging questions, requiring your child to form a judgment and justify it using evidence from the passage. There is usually no single "right" answer — but the response must be logical and supported.

Structure to use: State your view clearly → Quote or reference evidence from the passage → Explain how the evidence supports your view. Example: "Yes, I think James made the right decision because the passage states that '___'. This shows that ___, which means ___."

General Comprehension Habits That Improve Scores

1. Read the questions before the passage

Skim the questions first so you know what to look for. When you read the passage, you will naturally pay attention to the relevant parts rather than trying to absorb everything equally.

2. Always answer in complete sentences

Students lose marks for fragments. Even if the question asks for one fact, the answer should be a proper sentence: "The boy went to the park because he wanted to meet his friends" — not "To meet his friends."

3. Use the marks as a guide

A 2-mark question requires two points. A 3-mark question requires three — or one point with substantial explanation and evidence. Count the marks before you write your answer.

4. Quote from the passage when asked

For vocabulary questions or questions that ask "what does the writer mean by ___", use quotation marks and reference specific lines or phrases. Do not paraphrase when the question specifically asks for the writer's words.

5. Build vocabulary actively

Comprehension passages in PSLE use a wide range of vocabulary — including figurative language, idioms, and descriptive phrases. Regular reading (fiction, non-fiction, newspaper articles) builds the vocabulary bank students need to understand unfamiliar passages under exam conditions.

What parents can do at home

  • Read with your child and discuss what they read — not just the plot, but what characters were feeling and why
  • When they encounter an unfamiliar word, look it up together and use it in a sentence
  • Ask open-ended questions: "Why do you think the character did that?" — this builds the inferential thinking muscle
  • Encourage exposure to different text types: news articles, biographies, adventure fiction, informational texts

Common Mistakes in PSLE Comprehension

  • Copying too much from the passage — lift relevant phrases, not entire paragraphs
  • Not answering what was asked — re-read the question after writing your answer to check it actually responds to what was asked
  • Giving vague answers for inferential questions — "He felt sad" is not enough; explain why and cite the evidence
  • Ignoring the mark allocation — a 3-mark question answered in one sentence is a common way to lose marks
  • Rushing the passage — comprehension rewards careful, slow reading; speed-reading increases the chance of missing implied details
